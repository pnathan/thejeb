\begin{abstract}
The feasible set of a set theoretic estimation (STE) problem
\citep{combettes1993} lives in $\mathcal{P}(\Xii)$, and when the
hypothesis space is a product over $N$ variables, $|\Xii|$ is
exponential in $N$: representing the feasible set extensionally hits a
$2^N$ wall that is machine-checked in this project
(\lean{Ste.DynamicFrame.Model.card\_allExactStates}).  This note asks
when the feasible set can be represented \emph{locally} and glued, and
answers three pieces of the question with Lean-verified results in the
module \lean{Ste.Sheaf}.  (i) \emph{Gluing on the constraint side}: the
assignment $J \mapsto \feas(J)$ from constraint subsets to partial
feasible sets sends unions to intersections
(\lean{partialFeasibilitySet\_iUnion}), so any cover of the constraint
index recovers the global feasible set as the limit of local ones
(\lean{feasibilitySet\_eq\_iInter\_cover}); on this side there is no
obstruction.  (ii) \emph{A tractable upper bound}: if every constraint is
variable-separable (a rectangle), the feasible set is itself a rectangle
(\lean{rectangular\_feasibilitySet}) representable by one subset per
variable---$\sum$-space, not $\prod$-space---with product cardinality
(\lean{rectangular\_encard}).  (iii) \emph{The obstruction}: a single
coupling constraint, the diagonal on two bits, is provably not a
rectangle (\lean{diagonal\_not\_rectangular}).  This is the elementary
form of the Abramsky--Brandenburger contextuality obstruction
\citep{abramsky2011sheaf}, and the same shape as this project's
non-transitivity countermodel
(\lean{Ste.DynamicFrame.Counterexample}).  Four further Lean modules
push toward decomposition: \lean{Ste.Decomposition} proves that
disconnected constraint blocks give a product feasible set with
multiplicative cost (\lean{feasibilitySet\_blockFamily},
\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}); \lean{Ste.Support} gives
constraints a scope and shows the diagonal's scope is exactly
$\{0,1\}$ (\lean{diagonal\_support\_full});
\lean{Ste.Conditioning} mechanizes variable elimination---conditioning
commutes with feasibility, shrinks scopes, preserves rectangles, and,
applied to a \emph{cut} variable, disconnects the problem into
independent blocks
(\lean{piecewise\_mem\_condition\_feasibilitySet}); and
\lean{Ste.Treewidth} proves the general cut-elimination theorem
(\lean{cut\_elimination}), the join-scope accounting behind bucket
elimination, the quantitative \emph{bag bound}: the table of a
constraint on a scope $\sigma$ has at most $\prod_{v \in \sigma}
|A_v| \le k^{w+1}$ rows (\lean{table\_encard\_le},
\lean{elimination\_step}), and the \emph{elimination-order total-space
bound}: $n$ bags of width $w$ cost at most $n \cdot k^{w+1}$ total
table rows (\lean{elimination\_order\_total\_bound}).
\lean{Ste.Elimination} then mechanizes \emph{running} an order: the
bucket-elimination fold on lists of scoped constraints is correct
(\lean{joinConstraint\_bucketEliminate}), a complete order decides the
substituted problem (\lean{bucketEliminate\_decides}), and a width-$w$
run of $n$ steps materializes at most $n \cdot k^{w+1}$ rows in total
(\lean{bucketEliminate\_total\_space}).  \lean{Ste.Treedecomp}
discharges the width hypothesis: the \emph{induced treewidth} of an
instance is defined as the least achievable elimination width, proven
well-defined and \emph{attained} by a concrete optimal order
(\lean{achievesWidth\_inducedTreewidth}), so some complete order
decides the substituted problem in $n \cdot
k^{\operatorname{tw}(B)+1}$ total table space \emph{unconditionally}
(\lean{bucketEliminate\_treewidth\_bound}).  A breadth-first sweep then
closes six further leads, each machine-checked: projective
($\exists$-)elimination decides feasibility \emph{unconditionally}
(\lean{projectEliminate\_decides\_feasibility}); elimination width
equals tree-decomposition treewidth in \emph{both} directions
(\lean{treewidth\_primalGraph\_eq}, over a from-scratch
Robertson--Seymour tree-decomposition datatype); the block law becomes
an $n$-ary product (\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blocks}); the
variable-side presheaf glues for rectangles and provably fails for the
coupling (\lean{rectangular\_glue}, \lean{diagonal\_gluing\_fails}); the
\v{C}ech obstruction is computed ($0$ for rectangles, $2$ for the
diagonal, \lean{diagonal\_cechObstruction}); and the junction-tree
representation is polynomial-size and faithful
(\lean{junctionTree\_size\_le}).  A second sweep then closes the
dichotomy's hard side --- singleton-cover gluing succeeds \emph{iff} the
constraint is a rectangle (\lean{cechVanishes\_iff\_rectangular}) and the
$n$-fold coupling has \v{C}ech obstruction $\ge 2^n - 2$ against a
constant-size feasible set (\lean{cechObstruction\_allEqual\_ge}), so
unbounded coupling is exponential where bounded width is linear --- and
mechanizes Freuder's width-1 tractability (a nonempty arc-consistent
chain is solvable, \lean{arcConsistent\_chain\_solvable}).  A third
sweep pushes into the documented open questions: the sheaf/gluing level
of the \v{C}ech obstruction is lifted to \emph{arbitrary} covers
(\lean{rectangular\_cechVanishesCover}: a rectangle's local sections
glue to a unique global section over any cover, non-vacuously ---
\lean{diagonal\_not\_cechVanishesCover}), and Freuder's tractability is
extended from chains to arbitrary rooted \emph{trees}
(\lean{treeArcConsistent\_solvable}).  A fourth sweep builds the full
linearized $\check{H}^1$ cochain complex over a module --- coboundaries
with $d \circ d = 0$, cohomology $\ker/\operatorname{im}$, exactness and
$\check{H}^1 = 0$ for the constant nerve, and the section-linearization
bridge (\lean{Ste.CechComplex}) --- and the higher-width tractability
theorem: directional (bucket) consistency along an elimination order
with separators of \emph{any} size yields a global solution
(\lean{directionalConsistent\_solvable}), with \lean{twoParent\_solvable}
the genuinely cyclic width-$2$ case.  A final pair of PhD-level studies
then pushes into the two hardest items, each with a machine-checked
partial result.  For the graded obstruction (\lean{Ste.TwistedCech}): a
\emph{twisted}, section-presheaf-valued \v{C}ech complex over which the
classic per-section Abramsky--Mansfield--Barbosa $\check{H}^1$ obstruction
provably \emph{degenerates} (\lean{amb\_extension\_always}: a false
negative), while a twisted degree-$0$ cokernel invariant \emph{does}
detect the coupling (\lean{diagonal\_twisted\_obstruction\_detects},
nonzero for the diagonal, zero for rectangles) --- one degree below the
AMB class.  For adaptive consistency (\lean{Ste.BucketConsistency}):
projective bucket elimination is proved to preserve the solution set and
admit backtrack-free extraction
(\lean{exists\_extension\_of\_mem\_projectBucketEliminate}), the relative
(Dechter) directional consistency it actually establishes is proved
sufficient (\lean{bucketRelativeConsistent\_solvable}), and the bucket
separators are tied to the induced treewidth (\lean{runSep\_link}).  The
\emph{quantitative} obstruction-size $=$ representation-blow-up equality
is now settled --- as a negative result with a mechanized replacement
(\lean{Ste.RepresentationBounds}, Section~\ref{sec:repbounds}): reading blow-up as the
rectangle-cover complexity $\rho(T)$ (the smallest number of product sets
whose exact union is $T$, the $C^1$ cover number of nondeterministic
communication complexity \citep{kushilevitz1997communication}), the
equality and \emph{both} uniform inequalities against the raw Čech
obstruction \emph{fail}
(\lean{not\_forall\_rectCoverNumber\_le\_cechObstruction},
\lean{not\_forall\_cechObstruction\_le\_rectCoverNumber}); what holds
instead is the fooling sandwich $\mathrm{fool}(T) \le \rho(T) \le |T|$
with $\rho(T) = 1$ iff the obstruction vanishes and
$\rho(\mathrm{allEqual}) = |\alpha|$ exactly.  The
``adaptive consistency is a complete, width-bounded solver'' theorem is
now also mechanized (\lean{Ste.AdaptiveSolver}): projective bucket
elimination on an order achieving width $w$ decides feasibility,
extracts a concrete solution backtrack-free, and stays within
$\operatorname{tw} \le w$, all in one statement
(\lean{projectBucketEliminate\_complete\_solver}); moreover the abstract
bucket network of \lean{Ste.AdaptiveConsistency} is realized as a scoped
list whose joint constraint is \emph{literally} the network's solution
set (\lean{joinConstraint\_bucketNetworkList}), so the solver operates on
it directly and returns a genuine \lean{AdaptiveSolution}
(\lean{adaptiveNetwork\_solved\_by\_elimination}) --- joining the two
formalisms in Lean.  What stays genuinely open is now narrower still:
constructing the width-$w$ elimination order from a separator width
bound, and efficient computation of treewidth, which is NP-hard
\citep{arnborg1987complexity,freuder1982backtrack,grohe2007complexity,dechter2003constraint}.
\end{abstract}

\section{The problem: the $2^N$ wall}

Combettes' set theoretic estimation \citep{combettes1993} specifies a
solution space $\Xii$, an index set $I$ of pieces of information, and a
property set $S_i \subseteq \Xii$ for each $i \in I$; the feasible
(solution) set is $S = \bigcap_{i} S_i$.  The framework is silent about
\emph{representation}: it treats $S$ as a set, not as a data structure.

The silence becomes a cost the moment $\Xii$ is a product.  In the
estimation problems this project cares about---dynamic frames over
documents, constraint grammars, hyperframe lattices---the hypothesis
space factors as $\Xii = \prod_{v \in V} A_v$ over a family of
variables (claims, mentions, cells), and $|\Xii| = \prod_v |A_v|$ is
exponential in $|V|$.  The finite solver in
\lean{Ste.DynamicFrame.FiniteSolver} makes the wall precise and
machine-checked: over $N$ ambient worlds there are exactly $2^N$
extensional states (\lean{Model.card\_allExactStates}), and crisp STE
has full subset expressivity---a single property set can carve out
\emph{any} subset of the universe
(\lean{arbitrary\_subset\_as\_single\_property})---so without structural
assumptions on the constraints, no representation smaller than $2^N$
can be complete.

The question of this note: \emph{which structural assumptions buy which
representations?}  The natural language for the question is
sheaf-theoretic, because ``represent locally and glue'' is exactly what
a sheaf condition asserts.

\section{Gluing over the constraint poset}

Fix $S : I \to \mathcal{P}(\Xii)$ and write, for a subset
$J \subseteq I$ of the constraint index,
\[
  \feas(J) \;=\; \bigcap_{i \in J} S_i
  \qquad (\text{\lean{partialFeasibilitySet}~$S$~$J$}),
\]
the set of hypotheses consistent with the information in $J$.  The
assignment $J \mapsto \feas(J)$ is a presheaf on the poset
$\mathcal{P}(I)$ (ordered by inclusion, mapping contravariantly): if
$J \subseteq K$ then $\feas(K) \subseteq \feas(J)$, which is the
information-monotonicity lemma
\lean{partialFeasibilitySet\_antitone} already verified in
\lean{Ste.Basic}.

The new, machine-checked content of \lean{Ste.Sheaf} is that this
presheaf satisfies the gluing condition exactly, with no sheafification
needed.  The engine is the Formal Concept Analysis bridge of
\lean{Ste.HyperFrame} \citep{ganter1999fca}: partial feasibility is
definitionally the lower polar of the satisfaction context
(\lean{partialFeasibilitySet\_eq\_lowerPolar}), and polars turn unions
into intersections.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{partialFeasibilitySet\_iUnion}]
For any family $\mathcal{J} : \kappa \to \mathcal{P}(I)$ of constraint
subsets,
\[
  \feas\Bigl(\bigcup_{k} \mathcal{J}_k\Bigr)
  \;=\; \bigcap_{k} \feas(\mathcal{J}_k).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{feasibilitySet\_eq\_iInter\_cover}]
If $\bigcup_k \mathcal{J}_k = I$ is any cover of the constraint index,
then the global feasible set is the intersection of the local ones:
\[
  \feas(I) \;=\; \bigcap_{k} \feas(\mathcal{J}_k).
\]
\end{theorem}

In sheaf language: the functor $\feas$ sends colimits (unions) of
constraint sets to limits (intersections) of feasible sets, so
\emph{local consistency data over any cover glues uniquely to the
global feasible set}.  On the constraint side of the Galois connection
there is no obstruction whatsoever---this is a theorem, not a
heuristic.  Whatever makes STE hard does not live here.

\section{The tractable upper bound: rectangular constraints}

Where the hardness \emph{can} live is the variable side.  Take
$\Xii = \prod_{v \in V} A_v$.  Call a constraint \emph{rectangular}
(variable-separable, a cylinder in every coordinate) if it has the form
$\prod_{v} P_v$ for some family of per-variable sets
$P_v \subseteq A_v$---in Lean, $\lean{Set.univ.pi}\;P$.  A rectangular
constraint inspects each variable independently; it couples nothing.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{rectangular\_feasibilitySet}]
If every constraint is rectangular, $S_i = \prod_v P_{i,v}$, then the
feasible set is itself the rectangle whose $v$-th side is the pointwise
intersection:
\[
  \bigcap_{i} \prod_{v} P_{i,v}
  \;=\; \prod_{v} \Bigl(\bigcap_{i} P_{i,v}\Bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}

This is the representation theorem for the easy case: a fully
variable-separable STE problem is represented \emph{exactly} by one
subset per variable.  The representation lives in
$\sum_{v} |A_v|$ space---linear in the number of variables---never in
the $\prod_v |A_v|$ product space.  The $2^N$ wall does not exist for
rectangular constraint families.

The cardinality statement is checked as well, in terms of Mathlib's
extended natural cardinality $\mathrm{encard}$ (which handles infinite
sides gracefully):

\begin{theorem}[\lean{rectangular\_encard}]
For a finite variable set $V$ (\lean{Fintype}~$V$),
\[
  \Bigl|\bigcap_i \textstyle\prod_v P_{i,v}\Bigr|
  \;=\; \prod_{v \in V} \Bigl|\bigcap_i P_{i,v}\Bigr|,
\]
as extended cardinals.
\end{theorem}

So in the separable case even the \emph{size} of the feasible set is a
product of locally-computable sizes: consistency checking, counting,
and sampling all decompose per-variable.  This is the STE incarnation
of the trivial end of CSP tractability: a constraint network with no
edges is backtrack-free \citep{freuder1982backtrack}.

\section{The obstruction: coupling is not rectangular}

The lower bound is a single, minimal counterexample.  Let the
hypothesis space be two binary variables, $\Xii = (\mathrm{Fin}\,2 \to
\mathrm{Bool})$, and consider the \emph{diagonal} (coupling,
equality) constraint
\[
  \Delta \;=\; \{\, f \mid f(0) = f(1) \,\}
  \qquad (\text{\lean{diagonal}}).
\]

\begin{theorem}[\lean{diagonal\_not\_rectangular}]
There is no family $t : \mathrm{Fin}\,2 \to
\mathcal{P}(\mathrm{Bool})$ with $\Delta = \prod_v t_v$.
\end{theorem}

The proof is the two-line contextuality argument: $(0,0) \in \Delta$
and $(1,1) \in \Delta$, so any rectangle containing $\Delta$ has
$0 \in t_0$ and $1 \in t_1$, hence contains the mixed point $(0,1)$
---which violates the coupling.  The local (per-variable) data of
$\Delta$ is \emph{full}: projected to either coordinate, everything is
possible.  The constraint is invisible locally and exists only
jointly.

This is precisely the elementary form of the sheaf-theoretic
obstruction of Abramsky and Brandenburger \citep{abramsky2011sheaf}:
a family of perfectly consistent local sections (here: both values
possible at each variable) that admits no factorization through a
product---the ``global section'' data is not determined by, and not
recoverable from, the per-variable marginals.  In cohomological
terms (made precise only in the outlook below), the diagonal carries a
nonzero \v{C}ech gluing class relative to the cover of $V$ by
singletons.

It is also, structurally, the same countermodel this project already
uses for a different purpose: \lean{Ste.DynamicFrame.Counterexample}
refutes transitivity of possible-coreference
(\lean{maySame\_not\_transitive}) with a three-world model in which
pairwise-consistent local identifications fail to assemble into a
global one.  Pairwise consistency without global consistency is the
one phenomenon, seen twice.

Consequences for representation: since rectangles are closed under
intersection (\lean{rectangular\_feasibilitySet} again), no family of
rectangular constraints can ever express $\Delta$.  Coupling
constraints are therefore \emph{exactly} the constraints that force
representations beyond $\sum$-space; the $2^N$ wall of
\lean{card\_allExactStates} is built out of them.

\section{Block decomposition: $\sum$ across blocks, $\prod$ within}

The module \lean{Ste.Decomposition} proves the first positive
decomposition result beyond full separability.  Suppose the hypothesis
space splits into two blocks, $\Xii = X \times Y$, and the constraint
family is indexed by a disjoint union $I = \iota \sqcup \kappa$ where
$\iota$-constraints depend only on the $X$ coordinate and
$\kappa$-constraints only on $Y$ (in Lean, \lean{blockFamily}~$C$~$D$
takes preimages under the two projections).

\begin{theorem}[\lean{feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}]
For any block families $C : \iota \to \mathcal{P}(X)$ and
$D : \kappa \to \mathcal{P}(Y)$,
\[
  \feas(\mathrm{blockFamily}\;C\;D)
  \;=\; \feas(C) \times \feas(D).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blockFamily},
\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blockFamily\_le}]
Consequently the representation cost multiplies,
$|\feas(\mathrm{blockFamily}\;C\;D)| = |\feas(C)| \cdot |\feas(D)|$
as extended cardinals, and is bounded by $|X| \cdot |Y|$.
\end{theorem}

Iterated, this is the $\sum$-across-blocks, $\prod$-within-block law:
when the constraint hypergraph is disconnected, the exponential lives
only inside each connected component.  The question becomes: what
counts as a block, and what dissolves coupling between blocks?  Both
answers need a machine-checked notion of \emph{which variables a
constraint actually touches}.

\section{Support: the scope of a constraint}

The module \lean{Ste.Support} defines, for a constraint
$T \subseteq \prod_v A_v$ and a set of variables
$\sigma \subseteq V$, the predicate \lean{HasSupport}~$T$~$\sigma$:
any two assignments agreeing on $\sigma$ are both in or both out of
$T$.  This is the scope of a constraint in the CSP sense
\citep{dechter2003constraint}, i.e.\ the hypergraph edge on which the
constraint sits.  Verified basic theory: supports are monotone
(\lean{HasSupport.mono}), every constraint is supported on $V$
(\lean{hasSupport\_univ}), support $\emptyset$ holds exactly for the
trivial constraints $\Xii$ and $\emptyset$
(\lean{hasSupport\_empty\_iff}) --- scope-free information is no
information --- rectangles are supported on their non-trivial
coordinates (\lean{hasSupport\_pi}), and supports are stable under
intersection, so a family with common scope $\sigma$ has feasible set
with scope $\sigma$ (\lean{HasSupport.inter},
\lean{hasSupport\_feasibilitySet}).

The payoff is a sharpening of the coupling obstruction of
Section~4 from ``not rectangular'' to ``scope provably $\{0,1\}$'':

\begin{theorem}[\lean{diagonal\_support\_full},
\lean{diagonal\_hasSupport\_pair}]
Every support of the diagonal $\Delta$ contains both variables, and
$\{0,1\}$ is a support; hence the scope of $\Delta$ is exactly
$\{0,1\}$.  In particular $\Delta$ has no support $\emptyset$,
$\{0\}$, or $\{1\}$
(\lean{diagonal\_not\_hasSupport\_empty},
\lean{diagonal\_not\_hasSupport\_zero},
\lean{diagonal\_not\_hasSupport\_one}).
\end{theorem}

Where \lean{diagonal\_not\_rectangular} says $\Delta$ is not a product
of per-variable data, this says more: no proper subset of the
variables suffices to decide membership.  The coupling core is
genuinely two-variable --- it is an irreducible edge of the constraint
hypergraph.

\section{Conditioning: variable elimination, machine-checked}

The module \lean{Ste.Conditioning} mechanizes the elimination step of
bucket elimination \citep{dechter2003constraint}: conditioning a
constraint $T$ on fixing one coordinate $v := a$,
\[
  \mathrm{cond}(T, v, a) \;=\;
  \{\, f \mid f[v \mapsto a] \in T \,\}
  \qquad (\text{\lean{condition}}).
\]
Machine-checked facts:

\begin{theorem}[\lean{condition\_feasibilitySet}]
Conditioning commutes with feasibility:
$\mathrm{cond}(\feas(S), v, a) = \feas(i \mapsto
\mathrm{cond}(S_i, v, a))$.  Elimination may be performed
constraint-by-constraint.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{HasSupport.condition}]
Elimination shrinks scopes: if $T$ has support $\sigma$ then
$\mathrm{cond}(T, v, a)$ has support $\sigma \setminus \{v\}$.  The
eliminated variable leaves the constraint hypergraph.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{condition\_pi};
\lean{condition\_condition\_self}, \lean{condition\_condition\_comm}]
The rectangular class is closed under conditioning on a feasible
value (the $v$-th side becomes trivial), and elimination is
idempotent per variable and commutes across distinct variables ---
the elimination order is immaterial.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{condition\_diagonal\_rectangular},
\lean{condition\_diagonal\_hasSupport\_one}]
Conditioning the diagonal on either value of variable $0$ yields a
\emph{rectangular} constraint of scope $\{1\}$ --- although the
diagonal itself is provably not rectangular and provably has no
support $\{1\}$.  Fixing one variable of the coupling core moves it
from the intractable class to the tractable one.
\end{theorem}

Finally, the cut-variable theorem connects elimination to the block
decomposition of Section~5.  Say the family $S$ is a \emph{block
family} for the partition $\{\sigma, \sigma^{\mathsf{c}}\}$ if every
constraint has support inside $\sigma$ or inside
$\sigma^{\mathsf{c}}$; and say $v$ is a \emph{cut variable} if every
constraint has support inside $\sigma \cup \{v\}$ or
$\sigma^{\mathsf{c}} \cup \{v\}$ --- the two sides interact only
through $v$.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{piecewise\_mem\_feasibilitySet}]
The feasible set of a block family is closed under block-wise
recombination: if $f, g \in \feas(S)$ then the splice
$\sigma.\mathrm{piecewise}\,f\,g$ (equal to $f$ on $\sigma$, to $g$
off $\sigma$) is again in $\feas(S)$.  This is the pi-space form of
``$\feas(S)$ is a product of two independent subproblems''
(cf.\ \lean{feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}).
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{condition\_cut\_blocks},
\lean{piecewise\_mem\_condition\_feasibilitySet}]
Conditioning on a cut variable disconnects the problem: after fixing
$v := a$, every conditioned constraint has support inside a single
block, and the conditioned feasible set
$\mathrm{cond}(\feas(S), v, a)$ is closed under block-wise
recombination.
\end{theorem}

This is variable elimination confining the exponential: eliminating a
cut vertex of the constraint hypergraph splits the remaining problem
into independent subproblems, each paying only its own product cost.
The next section makes the cut theorem general and the cost per bag
quantitative.

\section{Cut elimination and treewidth: the bag invariant}

The module \lean{Ste.Treewidth} proves, over an abstract variable type
$V$ with alphabets $A_v$, the two accounting facts that underlie
treewidth bounds \citep{dechter2003constraint}, then the quantitative
per-bag cost.

\subsection*{Scope accounting}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{HasSupport.inter\_union};
\lean{HasSupport.iInter}]
Joins union scopes: if $T$ has support $\sigma$ and $U$ has support
$\tau$, then $T \cap U$ has support $\sigma \cup \tau$; for a family,
$\bigcap_i T_i$ has support $\bigcup_i \sigma_i$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{condition\_inter}]
Conditioning distributes over intersection:
$\mathrm{cond}(T \cap U, v, a) = \mathrm{cond}(T, v, a) \cap
\mathrm{cond}(U, v, a)$.  Elimination commutes with joining.
\end{theorem}

With \lean{HasSupport.condition} (elimination removes $v$ from every
scope), the scope of an intermediate table of bucket elimination is
$(\bigcup_i \sigma_i) \setminus \{v\}$ --- the \emph{bag} of the step.

\subsection*{Cut elimination, general form}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{cut\_elimination}]
If $T, U$ have supports $\sigma, \tau$ with $\sigma \cap \tau
\subseteq \{v\}$ (the constraints share only the cut variable $v$),
then for any value $a$ the conditioned constraints
$\mathrm{cond}(T, v, a)$, $\mathrm{cond}(U, v, a)$ have
\emph{disjoint} supports $\sigma \setminus \{v\}$ and
$\tau \setminus \{v\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{inter\_nonempty\_iff\_of\_disjoint\_support};
\lean{condition\_inter\_nonempty\_iff}]
Disjoint scopes make joint feasibility independent: for supports
$\sigma \perp \tau$, $T \cap U \neq \emptyset$ iff $T \neq \emptyset$
and $U \neq \emptyset$ (splice the witnesses along $\sigma$).
Consequently, if $T, U$ share only the cut variable $v$, then after
conditioning on $v$ the joint problem is satisfiable iff each side is
satisfiable separately.  This is the pi-space form of the block
product \lean{feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}: fixing a cut variable
dissolves the join into independent checks.
\end{theorem}

\subsection*{The bag bound}

For a scope $\sigma \subseteq V$, the \emph{table} of a constraint $T$
is its restriction image
$\mathrm{table}(\sigma, T) = \{\, f|_\sigma \mid f \in T \,\}$
(\lean{table}) --- the relation bucket elimination materializes.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{HasSupport.eq\_preimage\_table}]
A constraint with support $\sigma$ is the preimage of its
$\sigma$-table: the table is a faithful representation, losing no
information.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{table\_encard\_le};
\lean{table\_encard\_le\_pow}]
For finite $\sigma$ and finite alphabets, the table of \emph{any}
constraint satisfies
$|\mathrm{table}(\sigma, T)| \le \prod_{v \in \sigma} |A_v|$; if
moreover $|A_v| \le k$ for all $v$ and $|\sigma| \le w + 1$, then
$|\mathrm{table}(\sigma, T)| \le k^{w+1}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{elimination\_step}]
The elimination step of bucket elimination---join constraints with
scopes $\sigma_i$, then eliminate $v$---produces a constraint that
(i) has support the bag $(\bigcup_i \sigma_i) \setminus \{v\}$,
(ii) equals the preimage of its bag table, and (iii) has bag table of
at most $k^{w+1}$ rows whenever the bag has at most $w+1$ variables
over alphabets of size at most $k$.
\end{theorem}

This is the per-step space invariant of variable elimination: an
elimination whose every bag stays within $w + 1$ variables never
materializes more than $k^{w+1}$ rows at a step --- the induced-width
cost $a^{w^* + 1}$ \citep{dechter2003constraint}.

\subsection*{The elimination-order total-space bound}

The global statement of bucket elimination sums the bag bound along a
whole elimination \emph{order}.  Presenting the order by the list of
its bags:

\begin{theorem}[\lean{elimination\_order\_total\_bound}]
Let $\mathrm{bags}$ be a list of finite bags, each of at most $w + 1$
variables, over alphabets of size at most $k \ge 1$.  Then the total
capacity of all bag tables satisfies
\[
\sum_{\sigma \in \mathrm{bags}} \; \prod_{v \in \sigma} |A_v|
  \;\le\; |\mathrm{bags}| \cdot k^{w+1}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{elimination\_order\_table\_total\_bound};
\lean{table\_encard\_le\_pow\_finset}]
For any list of realized steps $(\sigma_i, T_i)$ with
$|\sigma_i| \le w + 1$, the \emph{actual} materialized sizes satisfy
$\sum_i |\mathrm{table}(\sigma_i, T_i)| \le n \cdot k^{w+1}$ where $n$
is the number of steps.
\end{theorem}

This is the honest ``bucket elimination runs in $n \cdot a^{w+1}$
total space'' theorem, taking the order and its width as input.

\section{Running the order: the elimination fold, machine-checked}

The module \lean{Ste.Elimination} mechanizes \emph{running} a whole
elimination order.  An order is a list of $(v, a_v)$ steps;
\lean{eliminate} folds conditioning over it, and
\lean{condition\_eq\_self} shows conditioning outside the scope is
trivial (so a step only touches the bucket of its variable).

\begin{theorem}[\lean{HasSupport.eliminate};
\lean{mem\_eliminate\_iff}; \lean{eliminate\_eq\_univ\_or\_empty}]
Running an order removes every eliminated variable from the support:
the residue of a $\sigma$-supported constraint is supported on
$\sigma \setminus \mathrm{eliminated}$.  Elimination is substitution:
$f$ survives the fold iff $f$ overwritten with the eliminated values
lies in the original constraint.  Hence a \emph{complete} order (one
covering $\sigma$) leaves a decision: the residue is $\Xii$ or
$\emptyset$.
\end{theorem}

The full data-structure fold operates on a list of constraints with
finite scopes.  One \lean{bucketStep} at $(v,a)$ collects the bucket
of constraints whose scope contains $v$, joins it --- the join is
supported on the union of the scopes
(\lean{hasSupport\_joinConstraint}) --- conditions on $v$, and keeps
the rest untouched.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{bucketEliminate\_support};
\lean{bucketEliminate\_scope\_subset};
\lean{joinConstraint\_bucketEliminate}]
Along the whole run: every live constraint stays supported on its
recorded scope; every live scope avoids all eliminated variables; and
the joint constraint of the state is exactly the elimination fold of
the original joint constraint --- the data structure is a faithful
implementation of variable elimination.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{bucketEliminate\_decides}]
Run a complete order --- one whose variables cover every initial
scope.  Then every surviving constraint has empty scope, hence is
$\Xii$ or $\emptyset$, and the joint residue --- the full elimination
of the original joint constraint --- is itself $\Xii$ or $\emptyset$:
bucket elimination terminates in a decision.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{bucketEliminate\_total\_space}]
Let $\mathrm{bucketBags}$ trace the (bag, constraint) pair each step
materializes ($n$ steps materialize $n$ tables,
\lean{length\_bucketBags}, each faithfully tabled on its bag,
\lean{bucketBags\_support} with
\lean{HasSupport.eq\_preimage\_table}).  If every materialized bag has
at most $w + 1$ variables --- the order has width $w$ on this instance
--- then over alphabets of size at most $k$ the total size of all
materialized tables is at most $n \cdot k^{w+1}$.
\end{theorem}

Together: a width-$w$ elimination order decides feasibility of the
substituted problem in $n \cdot a^{w+1}$ total table space.  One
honest caveat, stated plainly: \lean{condition} \emph{substitutes} a
chosen value, so the fold decides the problem \emph{conditioned on the
chosen values} $(a_v)$ (\lean{mem\_eliminate\_iff} makes this exact).
The \emph{projective} step of bucket elimination --- existentially
quantifying $v$ rather than substituting it, so that a single run
decides unconditional feasibility --- is \emph{not yet proven}
(outlook), as are: a tree-decomposition datatype with its
junction-tree representation theorem, and the \lean{encard}
factorization of the conditioned feasible set through restriction maps
(the quantitative analogue of
\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}).

\subsection*{The width hypothesis discharged: induced treewidth}

The width $w$ above is a hypothesis on the \emph{given} order.
\lean{Ste.Treedecomp} removes it.  Say the instance $B$ \emph{achieves
width $w$} (\lean{AchievesWidth}) if some complete elimination order
--- one whose eliminated variables cover every scope --- materializes
only bags of at most $w + 1$ variables on $B$.  Over a finite variable
set with inhabited alphabets the predicate is satisfiable: the trivial
order eliminating every variable once (\lean{elimAll}) is complete
(\lean{eliminated\_elimAll}), and every bag it materializes is a
finite set of variables, so every instance achieves width $|V|$
(\lean{achievesWidth\_card}).

\begin{theorem}[\lean{inducedTreewidth};
\lean{achievesWidth\_inducedTreewidth}; \lean{inducedTreewidth\_le}]
The \emph{induced treewidth} $\operatorname{tw}(B)$ of an instance $B$
is the least achievable width.  It is well-defined (the minimum of a
nonempty set of naturals, $\operatorname{tw}(B) \le |V|$ by
\lean{inducedTreewidth\_le\_card}) and \emph{attained}: a concrete
optimal elimination order realizes it.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\lean{bucketEliminate\_treewidth\_bound}]
For every instance $B$ of scoped constraints over alphabets of size at
most $k$, there \emph{exists} a complete elimination order that
decides the substituted joint constraint --- the elimination residue
is $\Xii$ or $\emptyset$ --- while materializing at most $n \cdot
k^{\operatorname{tw}(B)+1}$ total table rows, $n$ the length of the
order.  The width hypothesis of \lean{bucketEliminate\_total\_space}
is discharged: the bound holds at the instance's own attained induced
treewidth, with no free width parameter.
\end{theorem}

Two former limits of this discharge, both now resolved.  First, the
substitution caveat --- that the run decides feasibility only
\emph{conditioned on the chosen values} --- is removed by projective
elimination (\S\ref{sec:projection}): eliminating by projection instead
of substitution decides \emph{unconditional} feasibility.  Second,
$\operatorname{tw}(B)$ is elimination width (Dechter's induced width
over all complete orders); its \emph{equivalence} with the
tree-decomposition treewidth of the primal graph is now \emph{fully}
mechanized in both directions (next section,
\lean{treewidth\_primalGraph\_eq}).  What remains genuinely out of
reach is only \emph{efficient} computation of $\operatorname{tw}(B)$:
the optimal order is obtained by minimization over a nonempty set, and
computing treewidth is NP-hard
\citep{arnborg1987complexity} --- a settled hardness result, not a gap
in this development.

\section{The graph-treewidth bridge, machine-checked}

The induced treewidth $\operatorname{tw}(B)$ of the previous section is
defined operationally, through elimination orders.  Its classical
counterpart is the Robertson--Seymour treewidth of the \emph{primal}
(co-occurrence) graph $G_B$ --- vertices are variables, and $u \sim v$
iff some scope of $B$ contains both.  The file \lean{Ste.GraphTreewidth}
builds the graph side from scratch (Mathlib has \lean{SimpleGraph} but
no tree decompositions) and proves one direction of the equivalence.

A \lean{TreeDecomposition} of a graph $G$ is a list of bags with a
strictly increasing parent function on positions, satisfying the three
Robertson--Seymour axioms: every vertex lies in a bag
(\lean{vertexCover}), every edge lies in a common bag
(\lean{edgeCover}), and running intersection
(\lean{runningIntersection}), stated in parent-closure form --- a vertex
in bag $i$ and in some later bag lies in bag $\operatorname{parent}(i)$.
For an increasing parent this is equivalent to the usual
``bags containing a vertex form a connected subtree'': the parent-walk
from any occurrence strictly climbs, stays among occurrences, and
terminates at the topmost one, which is their common ancestor.  The
width is the largest bag size minus one, and
\[
  \operatorname{treewidth}(G) \;:=\;
  \inf\{\, w \mid \exists\,\text{tree decomposition of width} \le w \,\},
\]
well-defined and attained because the single-bag decomposition
$[\,V\,]$ always exists over a finite vertex set
(\lean{TreeDecomposition.single}, \lean{treewidth\_le\_card}).

\begin{theorem}[\lean{primalGraph\_elimination\_cover}]
For every instance $B$ over a finite variable set with inhabited
alphabets there exists a list of bags that covers every variable and
every primal edge of $B$, each of size at most
$\operatorname{tw}(B) + 2$.
\end{theorem}

The bags are the \emph{elimination bags}: at each step of the optimal
order, the recorded message scope $\bagof{v}$ together with the
eliminated variable $v$ itself.  This is exactly the elimination clique
$\{v\} \cup \operatorname{nbr}(v)$ of the induced graph.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{treewidth\_primalGraph\_le}]
For every instance $B$ over a finite variable set with inhabited
alphabets,
\[
  \operatorname{treewidth}(G_B) \;\le\; \operatorname{tw}(B) + 1 .
\]
\end{theorem}

The proof exhibits a genuine tree decomposition of $G_B$ --- all three
axioms, running intersection included, discharged sorry-free.  The
construction dedupes the optimal order to a repetition-free one with a
sublist of bags (\lean{exists\_nodup\_order}, using that empty-scope
constraints are \lean{InertLe} for elimination), pads it to eliminate
every variable (the padding bags are empty, since the state is already
decided), and builds the decomposition recursively
(\lean{exists\_elim\_treeDecomposition}): the bag of each step is
$\{v\} \cup \bagof{v}$, and its parent is a later bag containing its
message scope.  Running intersection closes because a deduped order
never re-eliminates a variable, so each eliminated variable appears in
no bag above its own.

\subsection*{The offset is exactly one, and it is not an artifact}
$\operatorname{treewidth}(G_B) \le \operatorname{tw}(B) + 1$, not
equality of the two symbols, because they count \emph{different} things
by exactly one variable.  Our \lean{inducedTreewidth} records the
\emph{message scope} $\bagof{v}$, which excludes the eliminated
variable $v$; a Robertson--Seymour bag must include $v$ (otherwise the
edges incident to $v$ are uncovered).  So the elimination bag is one
larger than the message scope, and:
\[
  \max_v |\bagof{v}| \;=\; \operatorname{tw}(G_B)
  \quad\text{(Dechter's induced width)},
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tw}(B) \;=\; \max_v |\bagof{v}| - 1 .
\]
Read symbolically: the quantity equal to graph treewidth is
$\max_v |\bagof{v}|$ (the largest message scope), while our slack
parameter \lean{inducedTreewidth} equals $\operatorname{tw}(G_B) - 1$
for any coupled instance.  For a \emph{fixed} order the induced width is
$\ge \operatorname{tw}(G_B)$ (GTE); minimized over orders they are equal
--- the classical Bodlaender/Dechter theorem
\citep{bodlaender1998arboretum,dechter2003constraint}.

\emph{Both halves are now machine-checked.}  The forward (tractable)
half, \lean{treewidth\_primalGraph\_le} above, turns an elimination
order of width $w$ into a tree decomposition of width $\le w+1$.  The
converse --- the deep half, that no tree decomposition beats
elimination --- is \lean{Ste.TreewidthConverse}: from any tree
decomposition of width $w$ we \emph{extract} a complete elimination
order of message-scope $\le w$ by eliminating variables in ascending
order of their topmost bag position.  The combinatorial heart is the
chain lemma \lean{TreeDecomposition.mem\_top\_of\_shared\_bag} (if $u$
and $x$ share a bag and $\operatorname{top} u \le \operatorname{top} x$
then $x$ lies in $u$'s topmost bag --- the rooted-tree avatar of
running intersection), which drives
\lean{bucketBags\_card\_le\_of\_pairwise\_top}: eliminating in
ascending-top order keeps every message scope inside
$\mathrm{bags}[\operatorname{top} u] \setminus \{u\}$, of size $\le w$.
Hence \lean{inducedTreewidth\_le\_treewidth\_sub\_one}
($\operatorname{tw}(B) \le \operatorname{tw}(G_B) - 1$,
unconditional) and, whenever the primal graph has an edge,

\begin{theorem}[\lean{treewidth\_primalGraph\_eq}]
$\operatorname{tw}(G_B) = \operatorname{tw}(B) + 1$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The edge hypothesis is sharp: an edgeless instance has
$\operatorname{tw}(G_B) = \operatorname{tw}(B) = 0$, so the unconditional
statement is \lean{max\_treewidth\_primalGraph\_one\_eq}:
$\max(\operatorname{tw}(G_B), 1) = \operatorname{tw}(B) + 1$.  The
equivalence of Robertson--Seymour treewidth and bucket-elimination
induced width is thus fully mechanized, no longer outlook.

\section{Four more leads, run down}

The breadth-first sweep of the remaining ``outlook'' claims closed four
further leads, each machine-checked sorry-free.

\subsection{Projective elimination: unconditional feasibility}
\label{sec:projection}
The conditioning fold decides
feasibility only relative to the substituted values.  Projective
elimination (\lean{Ste.Projection}) removes that caveat.  Define
$\operatorname{project}(T, v) = \{f \mid \exists a,\ f[v \mapsto a] \in
T\}$: eliminate $v$ by projecting it out, not by fixing a value.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{project\_nonempty\_iff}]
$\operatorname{project}(T, v) \ne \emptyset \iff T \ne \emptyset$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The exists-step preserves nonemptiness \emph{exactly} (given
$g \in T$, take the value $g(v)$; the reverse is immediate).  Folding
over a complete elimination list therefore decides unconditional
feasibility (\lean{projectEliminate\_decides\_feasibility},
\lean{projectEliminate\_joinConstraint\_decides}): the residue is
$\Xii$ iff the instance is feasible and $\emptyset$ iff it is not, with
no chosen values.  The exists-step is the union of all conditionings
(\lean{project\_eq\_iUnion\_condition}), tying it to the existing
machinery.

\subsection{Quantitative factorization over a partition}
The binary block law generalizes to arbitrarily
many independent blocks (\lean{Ste.Factorization}).  For a finite index
$\iota$ and per-block constraints $C_i$, the feasible set is
$\prod_i C_i$ (as \lean{Set.pi}) and

\begin{theorem}[\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blocks}]
$\bigl|\operatorname{feas}\bigr| = \prod_{i} |C_i|$
\end{theorem}

\noindent so the exponential representation cost is confined to each
coupled block --- an estimator pays $\sum_i \operatorname{cost}(C_i)$
across blocks, not $\prod_i$ for the joint set.  This is the
$\sum$-across, $\prod$-within law made quantitative at the cardinality
level.

\subsection{The variable-side presheaf and its gluing dichotomy}
An earlier section proved gluing on the \emph{constraint} poset; the
open direction was the \emph{variable} side.  \lean{Ste.VariablePresheaf}
assigns to each variable set $W$ the local sections
$\operatorname{localSections}(T, W)$ with functorial restriction
(\lean{restrict\_restrict}), a genuine presheaf.  Its sheaf/gluing
condition over the singleton cover of $V$ \emph{succeeds} for
rectangular constraints --- \lean{rectangular\_glue} gives a
\emph{unique} global glue --- and provably \emph{fails} for the
coupling: \lean{diagonal\_gluing\_fails} exhibits local sections
($\mathsf{false}$ at $x$, $\mathsf{true}$ at $y$), each individually
extendable to the diagonal, that no global diagonal section restricts
to.  The glue of the diagonal's singletons has four points to the
diagonal's two (\lean{singletonGlue\_diagonal\_encard}).  For the
singleton cover the characterization is in fact an \emph{iff} ---
gluing succeeds precisely for rectangles
(\lean{cechVanishes\_iff\_rectangular}, next-but-one section); the
general-cover version (gluing precisely on the tractable fragments)
stays outlook.

\subsection{The concrete \v{C}ech obstruction}
Following \citet{abramsky2011sheaf}, the obstruction to gluing
compatible local sections into a global one is a \v{C}ech $\check{H}^1$ class.
\lean{Ste.CechObstruction} computes it concretely for the singleton
cover: with $\operatorname{compatible}(T)$ the rectangle of $T$'s
projections and $\operatorname{glued}(T) = T$, the obstruction is
$\operatorname{cechObstruction}(T) = |\operatorname{compatible}(T)| -
|\operatorname{glued}(T)|$.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{rectangular\_cechObstruction};
\lean{diagonal\_cechObstruction}]
The obstruction vanishes for every rectangular constraint ($\check{H}^1 = 0$)
and equals $2 \ne 0$ for the diagonal.
\end{theorem}

\noindent Vanishing forces rectangularity
(\lean{rectangular\_of\_cechVanishes}), bridging the cohomological
obstruction to the representation obstruction of the coupling.
The singleton cover is the finest; \lean{Ste.CechCover} lifts the
gluing level to an \emph{arbitrary} cover $U : J \to \mathcal{P}(V)$
with substantive overlaps.  A \emph{compatible family} assigns each
context $U_j$ a local section agreeing with its neighbours on overlaps
(the Čech cocycle condition); it \emph{glues} when it is the restriction
of a single global section.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{rectangular\_cechVanishesCover};
\lean{rectangular\_glueCover\_existsUnique}]
For a rectangular constraint and \emph{any} cover of the variable set,
every compatible family of local sections glues to a \emph{unique}
global section.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The glue picks, for each variable, a context containing it and
reads off the section there --- well-defined because overlap agreement
makes the choice immaterial.  This is a genuine sheaf theorem over
arbitrary covers, not just the singleton one; the singleton instance
recovers the earlier result exactly
(\lean{cechVanishesCover\_singleton\_iff}), and gluing provably
\emph{fails} for the diagonal (\lean{diagonal\_not\_cechVanishesCover}),
so the theorem has real content.

\subsection{The linearized cochain complex}
\lean{Ste.CechComplex} builds the module-level machinery
A--B use to \emph{grade} the obstruction.  Over any commutative ring $R$
and $R$-module $M$, the Čech cochains $C^0 = (\iota \to M)$,
$C^1 = (\iota \to \iota \to M)$, $C^2, C^3$ carry the alternating
coboundaries $d^0, d^1, d^2$ (e.g.\ $(d^0 f)_{ij} = f_j - f_i$), and

\begin{theorem}[\lean{cechComplex\_d\_comp\_d};
\lean{cechComplex\_d2\_comp\_d1}]
$d^1 \circ d^0 = 0$ and $d^2 \circ d^1 = 0$ --- a genuine cochain complex.
\end{theorem}

\noindent Cohomology is then the honest quotient of modules
$\check{H}^1 = \ker d^1 / \operatorname{im} d^0$ (and $\check{H}^2$ one
degree up); over a nonempty index type every cocycle is a coboundary
(basepoint primitive), so $\check{H}^1 = \check{H}^2 = 0$
(\lean{cechH1\_subsingleton}) --- the constant-coefficient full nerve is
an acyclic simplex --- while $\check{H}^0 \cong M$.  A global section
linearizes to a $0$-cocycle in the free module $(\forall v,\ A_v) \to_0
R$ (\lean{globalZeroCochain\_mem\_cechH0}), injectively
(\lean{globalZeroCochain\_injective}), and the set-level gluing of
the general-cover gluing above lands in this linearized $\check{H}^0$
(\lean{CompatibleFamily.exists\_cechH0}).  What remains genuinely open
is a \emph{nonzero graded} $\check{H}^1$ locating the coupling --- which
needs twisted (locally varying) coefficients or a non-full nerve, not
the constant ones vanishing here --- and the quantitative ``obstruction
size $=$ smallest-representation blow-up'' equality.

\subsection{The junction-tree representation}
\lean{Ste.JunctionTree} packages the bucket-elimination trace as one
projected bag table per eliminated bucket.  The representation is
\emph{faithful} --- each materialized constraint is the preimage of its
bag table (\lean{junctionTables\_faithful}) --- and

\begin{theorem}[\lean{junctionTree\_size\_le};
\lean{junctionTree\_size\_linear}]
Some complete order decides feasibility with a faithful bag-table
representation of total size $\le n \cdot k^{\operatorname{tw}(B)+1}$;
at fixed width $w$ and alphabet bound $k$ this is $n \cdot C$ with
$C = k^{w+1}$ a constant --- linear in the number of variables.
\end{theorem}

\noindent This is the tractable direction of the CSP dichotomy
\citep{freuder1982backtrack,grohe2007complexity,dechter2003constraint}:
bounded treewidth yields a polynomial-size exact representation of a
feasible set that may itself be exponential.  The matching lower bound
is the next section.

\section{The dichotomy's hard side: unbounded coupling is exponential}

The tractable direction above is only half a dichotomy without a
matching lower bound.  \lean{Ste.CouplingLowerBound} supplies the
concrete hard side.  First it upgrades the presheaf gluing dichotomy of
the variable-side presheaf (above) to a genuine \emph{iff} for the singleton
cover:

\begin{theorem}[\lean{cechVanishes\_iff\_rectangular}]
The \v{C}ech obstruction of the singleton cover vanishes ---
equivalently, every compatible family of local sections glues --- iff
the constraint is a rectangle $\mathrm{univ.pi}\,P$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent So coupling is \emph{exactly} the obstruction to
singleton-cover gluing.  Then it scales the two-bit diagonal to the
\emph{n-fold all-equal coupling} $\Delta_n = \{f : \mathrm{Fin}\,n \to
\alpha \mid \forall i\,j,\ f_i = f_j\}$ --- the maximal coupling, whose
primal graph is complete (induced width $n-1$).  Its feasible set is
tiny (\lean{allEqual\_encard}: $|\Delta_n| = |\alpha|$, one constant vector
per value), yet every margin is full, so:

\begin{theorem}[\lean{cechObstruction\_allEqual};
\lean{cechObstruction\_allEqual\_ge}]
$\operatorname{cechObstruction}(\Delta_n) = |\alpha|^n - |\alpha|$, and
for $|\alpha| \ge 2$ this is $\ge 2^n - 2$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The gap between the $|\alpha|$ genuine solutions and the
$|\alpha|^n$ margin-compatible families grows exponentially in the
number of variables (\lean{allEqual\_not\_rectangular},
\lean{allEqual\_not\_cechVanishes} record non-rectangularity at every
arity).  Set against \lean{junctionTree\_size\_linear}'s $n \cdot
k^{w+1}$ at bounded width, this is the STE form of the bounded-width
tractability dichotomy \citep{dechter2003constraint}, now machine-checked
on both sides: bounded coupling is linear, unbounded coupling is
exponential.

\section{Local consistency: the width-1 tractable case}

The constructive counterpart of bounded width is that a locally
consistent, acyclic instance is \emph{solvable} --- Freuder's
backtrack-free theorem \citep{freuder1982backtrack}.
\lean{Ste.Consistency} mechanizes the chain (width-1) case.  For a path
of variables $\mathrm{Fin}(n+1)$ with domains $D_i$ and binary
constraints $R_i$ between consecutive variables, \emph{arc consistency}
asks that every domain value have a forward and a backward support.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{arcConsistent\_chain\_solvable};
\lean{arcConsistent\_backtrackFree}]
A nonempty arc-consistent chain has a global solution $f$ with $f_i \in
D_i$ and $R_i(f_i, f_{i+1})$ for every edge; moreover \emph{every} value
of \emph{every} domain lies on such a solution --- extended rightward by
forward supports and leftward by backward supports, with no choice ever
retracted.
\end{theorem}

\noindent The solution set is the \lean{feasibilitySet} of the chain
constraints (\lean{chainSet\_eq\_feasibilitySet}), and arc consistency
is the statement that the two-variable edge sections cover the margins
--- the concrete, width-1 instance of ``$k$-consistency as higher
gluing.''

\lean{Ste.ConsistencyTree} then removes the path restriction: an
arbitrary rooted \emph{tree} (a parent function on the non-root nodes of
$\mathrm{Fin}(n{+}1)$ with the topological-order hypothesis
$\operatorname{parent}(i) \le i$, so the last node is always a leaf).
\emph{Directional} arc consistency asks that every value of a node's
parent domain have a supporting child value.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{treeArcConsistent\_solvable};
\lean{treeArcConsistent\_solvable\_from}]
A nonempty directionally-arc-consistent rooted tree has a global
solution $f$ with $f_i \in D_i$ and $R_i(f_{\operatorname{parent}(i)},
f_{i})$ across every edge; moreover every root value extends to one
(backtrack-free).
\end{theorem}

\noindent The proof peels the last (leaf) node, solves the truncated
tree by induction, and extends across the final edge by the parent's
already-assigned value --- the greedy root-to-leaf search of
\citet{freuder1982backtrack}, now for arbitrary acyclic width-1
structure (the chain is the special case
$\operatorname{parent}(i) = i$).

\lean{Ste.AdaptiveConsistency} then breaks the acyclic restriction.
Replace a node's single parent by a \emph{separator} $\operatorname{sep}(i)
: \mathrm{Finset}$ of earlier neighbours --- its induced-graph
neighbourhood, of any size --- with a bucket constraint on
$\operatorname{sep}(i) \cup \{i\}$.  \emph{Directional} (width-bounded)
consistency asks that, for every domain-respecting assignment to the
earlier nodes, node $i$ has a value satisfying its bucket.

\begin{theorem}[\lean{directionalConsistent\_solvable};
\lean{twoParent\_solvable}]
A nonempty directionally-consistent bucket network admits a global
solution.  In particular, when each node has \emph{two} earlier
neighbours and a ternary bucket constraint --- the shape a triangulated
cycle produces under elimination --- the cyclic width-$2$ network is
solvable.
\end{theorem}

\noindent This is Dechter's adaptive consistency / Freuder's
strong-$k$-consistency-plus-width theorem
\citep{freuder1982backtrack,dechter2003constraint}: $|\operatorname{sep}(i)|
\ge 2$ is exactly how bounded induced width $> 1$ and cyclic primal
graphs arise (fill-in), and the same leaf-peel induction --- reduced to
Dechter's prefix-dependence to kill the separator bookkeeping --- closes
it; the tree is the width-$1$ special case
(\lean{treeArcConsistent\_solvable\_of\_adaptive}).  One subtlety made
honest in the mechanization: directional consistency is vacuous when a
later domain is empty, so the theorem carries a nonemptiness hypothesis
(the faithful root-value analogue holds for Dechter's stronger
earlier-variables-only \lean{PrefixConsistent}).  The \emph{enforcement}
half --- that projective bucket elimination \emph{establishes} the
recorded condition while preserving the solution set, and that the run
is a complete, width-bounded solver that extracts a genuine
\lean{AdaptiveSolution} --- is now mechanized in \lean{Ste.AdaptiveSolver}
(\lean{adaptiveNetwork\_solved\_by\_elimination},
\lean{adaptiveNetwork\_complete\_solver}), the abstract network being
realized as a scoped constraint list whose joint constraint is exactly
\lean{adaptiveSet}.  What remains outlook is building the width-$w$
elimination order from a separator width bound, and the converse
(width-$\ge k$ networks where $k$-consistency fails).

\section{Obstruction size versus representation blow-up: a sandwich, not an equality}
\label{sec:repbounds}

The one quantitative conjecture at the heart of the cohomological
programme --- that the \emph{size} of the \v{C}ech obstruction measures
the \emph{representation blow-up} of a constraint --- is now settled, and
the answer is negative in the strong reading and precise in the true one.
Everything in this section is machine-checked in
\lean{Ste.RepresentationBounds}.

There are two readings of ``blow-up''.  The first is the \emph{box gap}:
the smallest rectangle containing $T$ is the product of its per-variable
projections, which is exactly $\mathrm{compatibleFamilies}(T)$, so
$\mathrm{cechObstruction}(T) = |\mathrm{box}(T)| - |T|$ holds by
unfolding (\lean{cechObstruction\_eq\_box\_sub\_encard}).  This is
definitional bookkeeping, not a representation theorem.

The second reading is the honest one: the \emph{rectangle-cover
complexity} $\rho(T)$, the least number of product sets whose exact union
is $T$ --- the $C^1$ cover number of nondeterministic communication
complexity read on the constraint as a $0/1$ tensor
\citep{aho1983notions,kushilevitz1997communication,yannakakis1991expressing}.
Here the conjectured equality \emph{fails}, and so does every uniform
inequality against the raw obstruction magnitude, in \emph{both}
directions (\lean{not\_forall\_rectCoverNumber\_le\_cechObstruction},
\lean{not\_forall\_cechObstruction\_le\_rectCoverNumber}): a nonempty
rectangle has obstruction $0$ but $\rho = 1$, while the coupling
$\mathrm{allEqual}$ on $n \ge 3$ variables over $|\alpha| = k \ge 2$
values has $\rho = k$ yet obstruction $k^n - k$ ---
exponentially larger.  The two invariants are incomparable.

What \emph{is} true is a sandwich, all mechanized:
\[
  \mathrm{fool}(T) \;\le\; \rho(T) \;\le\; |T|,
\]
where $\mathrm{fool}(T)$ is the fooling number --- the largest set of
points no sub-rectangle of $T$ can pair
(\lean{foolingNumber\_le\_rectCoverNumber}, via the pointwise bound
\lean{IsFoolingSet.encard\_le\_of\_hasRectCover}) --- and the upper bound
is singletons-are-rectangles (\lean{rectCoverNumber\_le\_encard}).  At the
bottom of the scale the cover complexity and the cohomology agree
exactly: for nonempty $T$, $\rho(T) = 1$ iff $T$ is a rectangle iff the
\v{C}ech obstruction vanishes
(\lean{rectCoverNumber\_eq\_one\_iff\_cechVanishes}), and $\rho(T) \ge 2$
iff the obstruction is nonvanishing (\lean{one\_lt\_rectCoverNumber\_iff}).
For the coupling the sandwich pinches to an exact value: the $k$ constant
vectors are a fooling set (\lean{isFoolingSet\_allEqual}), forcing
$\rho(\mathrm{allEqual}\ n\ \alpha) = |\alpha|$
(\lean{rectCoverNumber\_allEqual}) --- the \emph{true} representation cost
is linear in $k$, not the exponential $k^n - k$ the raw obstruction
reports.

The conjecture's plausibility is itself explained: at the minimal
nonvanishing instance, the two-variable Boolean diagonal
$= \mathrm{allEqual}\ 2\ \mathrm{Bool}$ (\lean{diagonal\_eq\_allEqual}),
obstruction and cover complexity coincide, both equal to $2$
(\lean{diagonal\_cechObstruction\_eq\_rectCoverNumber}).  The equality is
a coincidence of the smallest example --- it breaks already at
$\mathrm{allEqual}\ 3\ \mathrm{Bool}$ ($2 < 6$) --- not a law.  What
remains outlook is quantitative refinement, not the qualitative question:
the logarithmic relation
$\log \rho \le \text{communication complexity} \le \log \rho + O(\log|T|)$
\citep{kushilevitz1997communication}, nonnegative-rank / extension-complexity
sharpenings \citep{yannakakis1991expressing,fiorini2015exponential}, and cover
numbers for covers with nonempty overlaps.

\section{Outlook: the cohomological program (not proven)}

\emph{Everything in this section is conjecture or programme.  None of
it is machine-checked, and none of it should be cited as a result of
this project.}

\begin{conjecture}[Feasibility as a sheaf on a site over variables]
Section~2 proved gluing over the \emph{constraint} poset.  The
substantive open direction is the \emph{variable} side: assign to each
subset $W \subseteq V$ of variables the set of partial assignments on
$W$ consistent with all constraints whose support lies in $W$.  We
conjecture this is a presheaf on a Grothendieck site over
$\mathcal{P}(V)$ whose sheaf condition, relative to a cover of $V$ by
constraint supports, holds precisely for the tractable fragments; the
diagonal example shows the presheaf is not a sheaf for the singleton
cover in general.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[$k$-consistency is higher gluing]
Local ($k$-)consistency in the CSP sense
\citep{freuder1982backtrack,dechter2003constraint}---every consistent
assignment on $k-1$ variables extends to any $k$-th---should be
exactly the statement that sections glue along the cover of $V$ by
$k$-element subsets.  Freuder's theorem (strong $k$-consistency plus
width $< k$ implies backtrack-free search) would then become: on
covers refined enough for the treewidth, the presheaf \emph{is} a
sheaf, and a global section is constructed greedily.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[$\check{H}^1$ measures intractability]
Following \citet{abramsky2011sheaf}, the obstruction to a family of
compatible local sections extending to a global one should be captured
by a \v{C}ech cohomology class in $\check{H}^1$ of the support cover
with coefficients in the (relative) presheaf of partial solutions; the
class vanishes iff gluing succeeds.  \emph{The quantitative version of
this conjecture is no longer open}: as Section~\ref{sec:repbounds}
establishes, the difficulty of an STE instance---the size blow-up of its
smallest exact representation $\rho(T)$---is \emph{not} controlled by the
raw magnitude of the obstruction in either direction; the correct
mechanized statement is the fooling sandwich
$\mathrm{fool}(T) \le \rho(T) \le |T|$, with rectangular families the
$\rho = 1 \Leftrightarrow \check{H}^1 = 0$ case of Section~3 and the
diagonal of Section~4 the minimal nonvanishing class.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Tractability dichotomy]
Bounded treewidth of the constraint (co)occurrence structure---or,
conjecturally equivalent in this setting, bounded obstruction in the
sense above---yields a polynomial-size glued representation
(junction-tree style: rectangles over bags, glued along separators),
and unbounded coupling reproduces the $2^N$ wall.  This would be the
STE form of the classical CSP dichotomy between bounded-width
tractability and general intractability
\citep{dechter2003constraint}.
\end{conjecture}

What stands \emph{proven} against this programme: the fully separable
case is exactly linear (\lean{rectangular\_feasibilitySet},
\lean{rectangular\_encard}); one coupling suffices to leave it, and
its scope is provably irreducible (\lean{diagonal\_not\_rectangular},
\lean{diagonal\_support\_full}); disconnected blocks multiply
(\lean{feasibilitySet\_blockFamily},
\lean{encard\_feasibilitySet\_blockFamily}); eliminating a variable
removes it from every scope (\lean{HasSupport.condition}); eliminating
a \emph{cut} variable disconnects the residual problem into
independent blocks with disjoint scopes
(\lean{piecewise\_mem\_condition\_feasibilitySet},
\lean{cut\_elimination}) whose joint feasibility factors into
independent checks (\lean{condition\_inter\_nonempty\_iff}); every
single elimination step is confined to its bag with table cost at most
$\prod_{v \in \mathrm{bag}} |A_v| \le k^{w+1}$
(\lean{elimination\_step}, \lean{table\_encard\_le}); a whole
width-$w$ order of $n$ steps costs at most $n \cdot k^{w+1}$ total
table space (\lean{elimination\_order\_total\_bound},
\lean{bucketEliminate\_total\_space}); and the bucket-elimination fold
itself is verified correct and terminating in a decision for the
substituted problem (\lean{joinConstraint\_bucketEliminate},
\lean{bucketEliminate\_decides})---the width-$1$ case of the
conjectured dichotomy, plus its space accounting, plus a verified
solver skeleton; and the width hypothesis is discharged: the induced
treewidth of an instance is well-defined and attained by a concrete
optimal order (\lean{achievesWidth\_inducedTreewidth}), yielding the
unconditional $n \cdot k^{\operatorname{tw}(B)+1}$ decision-and-space
bound (\lean{bucketEliminate\_treewidth\_bound}).  The
constraint-side gluing (\lean{feasibilitySet\_eq\_iInter\_cover})
guarantees the difficulty is genuinely about variables, not about how
information is indexed.  Open in this direction: projective
($\exists$-)elimination, the quantitative \lean{encard} factorization
of the conditioned feasible set over a partition, the equivalence of
elimination width with tree-decomposition treewidth, junction trees,
efficient (or any) computation of $\operatorname{tw}(B)$, and the full
treewidth dichotomy.
