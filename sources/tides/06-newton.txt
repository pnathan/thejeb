# Isaac Newton, Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, Book III (1687)
# Curated representative passage (public domain work; trans. after Motte). Provenance: refs.bib newton1687principia.

The flux and reflux of the sea arise from the actions of the sun and moon. By the theory of universal
gravitation, every body attracts every other with a force proportional to the masses and inversely as
the square of the distance; and so the sun and moon, by their gravitation, draw up the waters of the
sea. The moon's force to raise the sea is greater than the sun's, being nearer the earth. When the sun
and moon are in conjunction or opposition, their forces conspire, and the greatest, or spring, tides
are produced; when they are in the quadratures, the sun's force takes away from the moon's, and the
least, or neap, tides ensue. The heights of the tides answer in proportion to these forces, and may be
computed from the masses and distances of the two luminaries.
