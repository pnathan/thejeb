# Bede, De temporum ratione (The Reckoning of Time), ch. 29 (725 CE)
# Curated representative passage (public domain work). Provenance: refs.bib bede725temporum.

Concerning the concord of the sea and the moon. The ocean is the companion of the moon's
course: with her rising it comes on, with her setting it withdraws, and it follows her faithfully
by day and by night. As the moon each day rises later, so the tide each day comes later by the
like measure. Every shore has its own set time of high water, which the sailors know, appointed
according to the moon's place in the heavens. Twice in the lunar day the sea is drawn up and twice
let down; and at the new moon and the full the springs run highest, for then the moon's power over
the waters is greatest. The moon it is that lifts the waters and lets them fall.
