# Pliny the Elder, Naturalis Historia, Book II (c. 77 CE)
# Curated representative passage (public domain work). Provenance: refs.bib pliny77naturalis.

Much has been said of the nature of waters; but the most marvellous circumstance is the
alternate flowing and ebbing of the tides, which exists indeed under various forms, but is
caused by the sun and the moon. The tide flows and ebbs twice between each two risings of the
moon, always in the space of twenty-four hours. First, as the moon rises the seas swell, and
again they sink as she declines from the meridian; and after her setting they rise once more
until she comes again beneath the earth. The tides are greatest at the new and the full moon,
and least when the moon is at the half. Never are the waters moved but the moon draws them;
the sun's influence is joined to hers, adding to the swelling at the conjunctions.
