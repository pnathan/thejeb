# Johannes Kepler, Astronomia Nova, Introduction (1609)
# Curated representative passage (public domain work). Provenance: refs.bib kepler1609astronomia.

If two stones were placed anywhere in space near to each other, and beyond the reach of force of a
third cognate body, they would come together at an intermediate point, each approaching the other in
proportion to the other's mass. Gravity is a mutual bodily affection between cognate bodies towards
union. The moon, being cognate to the waters of the earth, attracts them; and the seas are drawn
toward the body of the moon by that same faculty. Were the earth to cease to attract its waters, all
the sea-water would be raised up and would flow to the body of the moon. The tides, therefore, are
the attraction of the waters by the moon. The earth's own attraction restrains them, but the moon
draws them upward as she passes overhead, and so the waters heap toward her.
