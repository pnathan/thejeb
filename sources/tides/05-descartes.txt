# Rene Descartes, Principia Philosophiae, Part IV, arts. 49-56 (1644)
# Curated representative passage (public domain work). Provenance: refs.bib descartes1644principia.

The earth is surrounded and carried along by a great vortex of the subtle celestial matter, and the
moon swims within this same fluid heaven. Because the space between the moon and the earth is not
sufficient to let the celestial matter pass freely, that matter is pressed and its motion straitened
beneath the moon. Hence the fluid heaven presses more strongly upon the waters lying under the moon,
and depresses them there, while raising the waters at the parts a quarter distant. As the earth turns
under the moon in the space of a day, this depression travels round, so the sea rises and falls twice
daily. It is not by any attraction, for I admit no attraction at a distance, but by the pressure of
the subtle matter that the tides are caused; and the sun, straitening the vortex likewise, augments
the effect at the new and full moon.
